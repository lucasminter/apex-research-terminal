Portfolio Metrics — Definitions
CAGR — Compound Annual Growth Rate Return Metric
CAGR is the single steady annual rate at which your portfolio would have grown to reach its final value, assuming returns compounded each year. It smooths out the year-to-year volatility into one representative number.

Formula: CAGR = (Final Value ÷ Initial Value)1/years − 1

A portfolio that grew from $10,000 to $32,000 over 12 years has a CAGR of approximately 10.1%, even if individual years ranged from −30% to +40%. CAGR is the gold standard for comparing long-term investment returns because it accounts for the compounding effect.
Volatility — Annual Standard Deviation Risk Metric
Volatility measures how widely annual returns are spread around the average. It is computed as the standard deviation of calendar-year returns across the backtest period.

A portfolio with 15% volatility and a 10% average return will, in a typical year, land somewhere between −5% and +25% (roughly ±1 standard deviation, ~68% of years). Roughly 95% of years fall within ±2 standard deviations (−20% to +40%).

Higher volatility is not inherently bad — it is the price of higher expected returns. The key is whether the extra risk is compensated with enough return (see: Sharpe Ratio).
Sharpe Ratio — Risk-Adjusted Return Efficiency Metric
Formula: (CAGR − Risk-Free Rate) ÷ Volatility  ·  Risk-free rate assumed: 3%

The Sharpe Ratio tells you how much return you are earning per unit of risk. Two portfolios can have identical CAGRs but very different Sharpe Ratios if one took twice the volatility to get there.

Interpretation:
< 0.5 — Poor · 0.5–1.0 — Acceptable · 1.0–2.0 — Good · > 2.0 — Excellent

A Sharpe of 1.0 means that for every 1% of annual volatility you accepted, you earned 1% of excess return above the risk-free rate. The S&P 500 has historically averaged a Sharpe of ~0.5–0.7 over long periods.
Max Drawdown — Worst Peak-to-Trough Decline Risk Metric
Max Drawdown is the largest percentage decline from a portfolio peak to a subsequent trough, measured on a monthly basis throughout the backtest. It represents the worst-case loss an investor would have experienced if they bought at the top and held through the bottom.

For example, a −50% drawdown means the portfolio fell by half from its peak before recovering. The S&P 500 experienced a −51% drawdown in the 2008–2009 Global Financial Crisis and a −34% drawdown in the 2020 COVID crash.

Why it matters: Many investors panic-sell during drawdowns. A strategy you can stomach through its worst decline is often more valuable than one with a marginally higher CAGR that causes you to exit at the bottom.
Best Year / Worst Year Return Range
The single calendar year in which your portfolio earned its highest (Best Year) and lowest (Worst Year) return. This shows the full range of annual outcomes you would have experienced historically and gives a gut-check on what extremes to expect.

For a portfolio heavily weighted toward equities, the worst year is often 2008 (Global Financial Crisis) or 2022 (inflation/rate shock). The best year for aggressive portfolios is often 2009 (recovery rally) or 2019–2021.
Total Invested & Net Gain Capital Metric
Total Invested is the sum of all cash you deployed: your initial lump-sum investment plus every recurring contribution over the backtest period. It represents your actual out-of-pocket cost.

Net Gain is the final portfolio value minus total invested — the portion of your ending wealth that the market generated on top of your contributions. This is the wealth created purely through compound growth, dividends, and reinvestment.
Expected Return %/yr — Projection Override Projection Setting
When running the Monte Carlo future projection, the simulator needs a forward-looking expected annual return. By default it uses your backtested CAGR, but this is automatically capped at 25%/yr to avoid extrapolating extreme historical periods (e.g., a portfolio with 30% Bitcoin may show a 60%+ historical CAGR).

You can manually lower this value for a more conservative projection. Long-run consensus estimates for US equities are typically 7–10% nominal, with international stocks slightly lower. Bonds are typically 4–5%, and cash/T-bills track the Fed Funds Rate.
Asset Classes — Data & Benchmarks
US Stocks S&P 500 Total Return Index · 1993–2024
Tracks 500 of the largest US-listed public companies, weighted by market capitalization. The S&P 500 Total Return Index reinvests all dividends, making it the standard benchmark for US large-cap equity performance. It covers approximately 80% of available US market capitalization across all 11 GICS sectors.

Data source: S&P Global / Slickcharts — verified calendar-year total returns 1993–2024.
Top 10 Holdings (by weight, ~2025):
Apple (AAPL) · Microsoft (MSFT) · NVIDIA (NVDA) · Amazon (AMZN) · Meta (META) · Alphabet (GOOGL) · Berkshire Hathaway (BRK.B) · Tesla (TSLA) · JPMorgan Chase (JPM) · UnitedHealth Group (UNH)
International Stocks MSCI EAFE TR Index (USD) · 1993–2024
Tracks developed-market equities in Europe, Australasia, and the Far East (EAFE), excluding the US and Canada. This index is the primary benchmark for international developed-market equity exposure. Returns are quoted in USD, so they incorporate currency fluctuations relative to the US dollar. Covers ~21 countries and ~800+ large/mid-cap stocks.

Data source: MSCI official fact sheets — verified calendar-year total returns 1993–2024.
Top 10 Holdings (by weight, ~2025):
ASML (Netherlands) · Nestlé (Switzerland) · Novartis (Switzerland) · LVMH (France) · Toyota (Japan) · Shell (UK) · Samsung Electronics (South Korea) · AstraZeneca (UK) · Novo Nordisk (Denmark) · HSBC Holdings (UK)
Bonds Bloomberg US Aggregate Bond Index · 1993–2024
The Bloomberg US Aggregate Bond Index ("the Agg") is the standard benchmark for the broad US investment-grade bond market. It includes US Treasuries, government agency bonds, mortgage-backed securities (MBS), and investment-grade corporate bonds — over 10,000 securities with maturities >1 year.

Composition (approximate): US Treasuries ~42% · Agency MBS ~26% · Investment-Grade Corporates ~25% · Other (CMBS, agency debentures) ~7%

Data source: Bloomberg / ICE BofA — verified calendar-year total returns 1993–2024.
Key Issuers (Investment-Grade Corporate segment):
US Treasury Notes & Bonds · Fannie Mae / Freddie Mac (Agency MBS) · Apple · Microsoft · JPMorgan Chase · Bank of America · Verizon · AT&T · Goldman Sachs · Wells Fargo
Real Estate (REITs) FTSE NAREIT All Equity REITs TR Index · 1993–2024
Real Estate Investment Trusts (REITs) are publicly traded companies that own income-producing real estate. They are required by law to distribute at least 90% of taxable income as dividends, making them a significant source of income in addition to price appreciation. The FTSE NAREIT All Equity REITs Total Return Index covers all US-listed equity REITs across all property sectors.

Data source: NAREIT (reit.com) — verified calendar-year total returns 1993–2024.
Top 10 Holdings (by market cap, ~2025):
Prologis (Industrial/Logistics) · American Tower (Cell Towers) · Equinix (Data Centers) · Crown Castle (Wireless Towers) · Public Storage (Self-Storage) · Welltower (Healthcare) · Digital Realty (Data Centers) · Simon Property Group (Retail Malls) · Realty Income (Net Lease) · SBA Communications (Towers)
Cash & Equivalents ICE BofA US 3-Month T-Bill Index · 1993–2024
Represents the risk-free rate of return via US Treasury Bills maturing in 90 days or less. T-bills are backed by the full faith and credit of the US government and are considered the nearest proxy to a risk-free asset in finance theory.

The rate moves closely with the Federal Reserve's Federal Funds Rate target. During near-zero rate eras (2009–2015, 2020–2022), cash returned essentially 0%. During tightening cycles (2022–2024), 3-month T-bills yielded 5%+.

Data source: FRED (Federal Reserve Economic Data) / ICE Data Services — verified calendar-year returns 1993–2024.
Real-world equivalents:
3-Month US Treasury Bills (direct purchase via TreasuryDirect) · Fidelity Government Money Market (SPAXX) · Vanguard Federal Money Market (VMFXX) · High-Yield Savings Accounts · Short-Term Treasury ETFs (BIL, SGOV)
Crypto (Bitcoin) BTC-USD Calendar-Year Returns · 2011–2024
Returns represent Bitcoin (BTC) close-to-close price change for each calendar year in USD, sourced from CoinGecko / CoinMarketCap. Pre-2011 returns are set to 0% — Bitcoin's Genesis Block was mined January 3, 2009, but there was no meaningful liquid market before 2011.

Volatility: Bitcoin's realized annual volatility is approximately 82% — roughly 5× that of the S&P 500. A small allocation (1–5%) can meaningfully impact portfolio skew due to its uncorrelated, high-return profile. Returns are highly leptokurtic (fat-tailed).

Data source: CoinGecko / CoinMarketCap — verified calendar-year close-to-close returns.
Bitcoin network metrics (~2025):
Market Cap: ~$1.8 trillion · Dominance: ~55% of total crypto market cap

Related public equities for Bitcoin exposure:
MicroStrategy (MSTR) · Coinbase Global (COIN) · Marathon Digital (MARA) · Riot Platforms (RIOT) · CleanSpark (CLSK) · Spot Bitcoin ETFs: iShares Bitcoin Trust (IBIT), Fidelity Wise Origin (FBTC)
